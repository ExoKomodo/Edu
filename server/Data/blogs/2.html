So ya finished the course. Nice! Good work!

Take a victory lap and a breather. That was respectable work, and I am sure it nearly crushed you. I'm also sure that you were told you'd be employable afterwards, and depending on your background, you may think that the piece of paper will get you the mileage you need in this career field. Well, I hate to break it to you, but every hiring manager I've spoken to since late 2022 has absolutely no interest in hiring bootcamp grads. In fact, I've spoken to more people who have company policy against this than people who are willing to discuss. And that leaves you, my dear friend, in a pickle. What are you going to do? Will you give up? Or will you learn to teach yourself, to feed yourself, to become independent, and prove the naysayers wrong? Every employer wants an employee that learns to deal with their problems without being spoon fed. Unfortunately, it's a tough status to achieve without a little guidance.

Which is where I am stepping in.

I am in the process of building out a full set of courses with the goal of moving people from the 'bootcamp grad' status to the world beyond, to having pride in their own work, to generating their own code from scratch, and having the confidence to say, 'I KNOW HOW TO CODE.' When I graduated two years ago, I had some confidence, but not a lot, and I had experience before I even started. So I can't imagine the average graduate feels good about their work, or that the curriculum did what it was supposed to; I can't imagine the average graduate feels like they paid $15,000 for a little more than what a youtube series would have taught them; and I'm gonna guess that, like me, most graduates at bootcamps are feeling a little sheepish, and extremely nervous about the whole process.

So I am working to fix this.

I want every bootcamp grad to get a job. I really do. And I really want each student to continue to grow, as they are told to, after they graduate. The problem is that most graduates are applying to jobs full-time, and they have discontinued their growth! They fall out of love with coding as fast as they fell in love with it, and we can't have that. Coding is beautiful! It's fun! It's a massive headache! It's the best of times and the worst of times!

So here is my big fat To-Do list, curated for the bootcamp graduate extraordinaire, or ordinaire, whichever one suits you.

First thing: I am assuming you have taken some full-stack coding coursework, and have worked with JavaScript and Python. I am also assuming you would like to work with full-stack tech; if not, try focusing on parallel or tangential technologies to the thing you want to learn. But if you want to understand the big picture of fullstack, my first recommendation would be to learn TypeScript. It writes very similarly to JavaScript (it is a 'superset!'), but it adds some clarifications. I also recommend watching Fireship “____ in 100 seconds” on YouTube for every topic you are planning to learn; his channel does a great job of giving you a 2-3 minute broad overview of what a tech topic is.
